# The Weekly Setup V2 — Digest

## Summary
The master overview lesson: it layers every element of the weekly business model into one narrative — FMWB at Sunday 5pm NY open, Asia setting the initial daily range, M/W formation at a session changeover, the 3-level trend with EMA targets, mid-week reversal on news Wednesday/Thursday, and the Friday take-profit / weekend-trap sequence. It also states the method's single biggest prohibition (no counter-trend trading after a level-1 rise) and the 4 valid retest conditions after level 1.

## Exact rules

### Week structure / timing (all times New York)
- [00:00] Week starts with a false move (FMWB, transcribed "FFWB") "sometime around 5pm New York" Sunday, when CME opens for the week. [00:30] It can happen earlier, or any time during Asia/UK/US on Monday; for crypto usually very close to market open.
- [00:30] Purpose of the false move: stop out/liquidate weekend traders still holding, and trigger breakout traders' pending orders to trap them.
- [01:00] After the false move completes, MM shifts price away from it; can become a one-session or even one-day trend.
- [01:00] Asia session sets the initial high and low of the day; "typically no more than a 2% range for Bitcoin".
- [01:30] If Asia runs wider than 2%: possible session shift that day → the whole day is unpredictable → learners should not trade that day at all.
- [02:00] No universal rule on whether initial HOD/LOD includes the dead zone; because the day starts 5pm NY, "we do include it in our system".
- [02:00–02:30] Provided Asia is typical, look for the M or W formation around the session changeovers — "the half hour gap between Asia to UK or between UK to US". The M/W usually follows the false move. Possible between dead zone and Asia, but not typical.
- [10:35–11:05] Mid-week reversal (MWR) expected "around Wednesday or Thursday for crypto", and "we generally will line this up with a news event" — use forexfactory.com.
- [12:36] Friday = take-profit day for the weekly setup. Typically sometime in the UK session a peak formation is created; if price is at your level 3, take profit there and "remove yourself from the weekend trap".
- [12:36–13:06] Friday UK session pattern (Fridays only): opens with an aggressive false move → slow trend for 4–6 hours → toward end of UK session, one more attempt to repeat the initial level from session open = the weekend trap.
- [13:36] After the trap, US session may continue or reverse the trend; around session end it consolidates into the weekend.
- [14:36] Not every week conforms; MM shifts session behaviours or runs a full "cycle reset" unexpectedly.

### M/W mechanics
- [02:30–03:00] M first peak traps longs; price shifts away and "eventually return close to it, but not exactly to it" — trapping more longs before the level-1 drop. Trapped longs' liquidation levels are the liquidity MM uses to run price down. Mirror-image for W.
- [03:00–03:31] Leverage ladder: level-1 drop liquidates ~100x traders; level 2 gets 50x; level 3 gets 25x. Below 25x leverage is "generally safe" unless the move is extremely extended.

### The 3-level trend
- [04:01] After the M/W early in the week, MM runs price through "3 levels or 3 days"; between levels there can be consolidation, a retracement, or nothing.
- [04:01–04:31] Level 1 must break the 50 EMA "with a high volume vector" — applies on any timeframe. A 50 EMA break lacking volume "is generally not counted as a level 1".
- [04:31] After breaking the 50 EMA, price generally attempts to get close to the 200 EMA (depending on distance); that's where the fast move is expected to end — start looking for a board meeting or retracement.
- [04:31–05:02] Rejection of the 200 EMA is a trade setup in the weekly cycle — but only depending on which level price is at.
- [05:02–05:32] THE major rule: if price has just completed level 1 and prints a reversal candlestick at the 200 EMA, that is NOT the 200-EMA-rejection setup — that's counter-trend trading. "We do not counter-trend trade after level 1 rise."
- [05:32] In a board meeting (sideways consolidation), assess the wicks (transcribed "wits") to see which side MM is trapping.
- [06:02–07:02] Four valid retest conditions after level 1 (any of, not just #1):
  1. Retest of the 50 EMA;
  2. Return to the vector that created the level-1 move;
  3. A higher low (after a W) / lower high (after an M);
  4. Return to a heatmap high-level order or a liquidation level (external software).
- [07:02] "Ideally, price will retrace to a combination of at least 2 of the 3 conditions" (she says "2 of the 3" though she listed 4 — inconsistency, see unclear passages).
- [07:02–07:33] It is OK for price to cross back over the 50 EMA during the retest, as long as it does not break higher than the second peak of the M / lower than the second peak of the W (that is the invalidation boundary).
- [07:33] Level-2 target: the 800 EMA if present; if not, a previous unrecovered vector; both together = added confluence.
- [08:03] Also layer heatmaps + liquidation levels — "the more arguments... the more confidence".
- [08:03–08:33] After level 2, again expect a board meeting or retracement — but in a very strong trend these can be skipped entirely between levels.
- [08:03–08:33] Level-3 target: can be an EMA on a HIGHER timeframe (200 or 800), ideally lined up with a previously unrecovered vector.
- [09:34–10:04] Target strength stacking: unrecovered vector + previous HOW/LOW or HOD/LOD + liquidation level or high-level heatmap order = "a very strong case".

### Reversal at level 3 → MWR
- [10:04] At level 3, look for candle wicks to switch sides — MM now trapping the opposite side.
- [10:04–10:35] At level 3, first find a stopping volume candle (SVC); assume traders are trapped there; wait for price to shift away, then wait for price to attempt to repeat that level.
- [10:35–11:05] If price falls short of repeating the exact level → you have an M or W → expect this is the mid-week reversal.
- [12:06] SVC example described: "that little tiny red candle after the big green one... high volume spike compared to the volume around it".
- [12:06–12:36] The retest that "couldn't get right back into that stopping volume candle zone" makes the second-peak entry safe; stop loss above the wick of the trap.

### Entries & stops
- [11:05] Aggressive entry: on the second peak of the M/W, stop loss clearing the first peak's wick.
- [11:05–11:35] Conservative entry: wait for the trend change — price breaks the 50 EMA with volume — then enter on one of the retest conditions. Stop above the second peak of the M/W; OR if there has been a stop hunt in the board meeting, the stop only needs to clear that stop hunt.
- [11:35] Rationale: once a stop hunt is issued, "damage is done" — MM shouldn't return to that level, so just clear of the stop hunt is a safe stop.
- [11:35] After MWR confirmation, expect another 3-level / 3-day trend (board meetings or retracements possible in between).
- [09:34] Stop-loss anchor for the whole weekly trade: "your stop loss in this example is always below the wick that creates the W" — even a deep pullback that stays a higher low vs. the W origin does not invalidate.

### Expectancy
- [15:06–15:37] Most strategies aim 1.5–2:1 R:R; catching the right M/W and holding all 3 levels yields minimum double that, often much more — one trade here ≈ 2–4 trades of other methods.

## Quantifiability

## New jargon (not in glossary)
- **Weekend trap / weekend box** — the Friday-UK-set trap held into the weekend.
- **Vector / high-volume vector** — coloured high-volume candle (from "vector candles"); **unrecovered vector** — one price hasn't returned through yet (used as targets).
- **Session changeover** — the half-hour gap between Asia→UK and UK→US.
- **Cycle reset** — MM unexpectedly restarting the whole weekly cycle.
- **Session shift** — day where sessions swap behaviours (flagged by Asia >2% range).
- **200 EMA rejection trade** — named setup within the weekly cycle.
- **Peak formation** — the swing extreme (used later as count anchor).
