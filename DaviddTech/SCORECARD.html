Eighteen candidate strategies from DaviddTech's public library — first five on BTC, then a six-strong ETH & SOL wave, then a seven-strong BTC bench wave that emptied the runners-up shelf — each frozen from its Pine source and put through a causal, cost-honest backtest on our own multi-million-candle datasets (BTC & ETH from 2019, SOL from 2020; in-sample through 2024, 2025–26 locked out). Survivors then faced an independent Jesse cross-check and Monte Carlo stress. Every number below is from our engine; his platform's figures only chose the candidates.

The BTC bench wave

Seven runners-up — pulled off the bench and tested

The original scorecard parked seven strong-looking strategies on a “bench.” This wave clears that shelf: each frozen from Pine, run through the same causal cost-honest engine and three-gate kill screen, then the survivors through an independent Jesse cross-check and Monte Carlo round (report DT25). Honesty

Read this before trusting any number above

  • The locked out-of-sample run carries a selection asterisk here. The library windows we picked from run into 2025–26, so choosing these five off his leaderboard already “peeked” at that period. Our primary evidence is the 2019–2024 in-sample test; when the one-shot 2025–26 run happens it must be read with that selection-bias caveat, not as clean out-of-sample proof.
  • The cross-check validates execution, not the idea. Jesse replaying our ledgers confirms our fills and cost accounting are right (all trade counts matched, tiny +0.01–0.04R deltas from a slippage difference). It does not independently re-derive the signal — it is a second engine running the same rules, so read it as “our numbers are computed correctly,” not “a second team found the same edge.”
  • His-engine numbers were never evidence. Every “his claim” column above is from his platform, his fills, his (sometimes leveraged) sizing. We used them only to choose candidates. The shrinkage is the point: PF 3.39→2.71, and a +1,451% headline that honestly is +0.28R per trade.
  • Multiplicity. We searched grids (27–48 cells each), so the single best cell is always flattered by selection. That is why each survivor's case rests on the whole positive surface (e.g. 48/48 cells green) and the center cell, not on a hand-picked champion.
  • DT3 and DT4 lean on their right tails. Both pass, but a handful of big winners carry them (DT3: top trade = 19% of R; DT4: top three = 68%). They are fat-tail bets. DT1 and DT2 are the more robust, broad-based survivors.
  • The ETH and SOL windows are shorter — especially SOL. ETH candles begin 2019-11 and SOL only 2020-09, so the SOL tests cover just ~4.3 years (one bull, one bear, one recovery) versus BTC's ~5+. A shorter, younger history means fewer independent regimes and more room for a single trend to dominate — read the SOL numbers with that in mind.
  • DT12's validation carries a parameter-shift asterisk. The strategy survives only in its 128-bar cycle region; the author's shipped 96-bar default fails Monte Carlo (16.2% risk-of-ruin). Choosing the deployable region is itself a selection decision, so DT12 is “validated with an asterisk,” not clean — and the 128-region's own best cell sits at, not comfortably above, the significance bar (t 2.23).
  • DT13 is largely SOL beta, not a symmetric edge. Its short leg is dead and ~100% of the expectancy is long-side dip-buying, 43% of it from the 2021 bull — i.e. much of the “edge” is just SOL going up 20×. That is why it is conditional and excluded from the combined account: a bear/sideways out-of-sample is the real test.
  • Leaderboard no-stop strategies flatter his platform's accounting. DT10 and DT11 look great on his site precisely because a no-stop system defers losses off the equity curve until a signal exit — TradingView's percent-of-equity accounting rewards the big deferred winners and hides the un-stopped tail. Our honest mark-to-market drawdowns (DT10 46%, DT11 100–212%) are the same strategies with the tail put back. The lesson: a strong leaderboard curve with no hard stop is a red flag, not a green one.
  • One edge can wear many costumes. The bench wave tested three more “different” trend strategies (Fugu, Regime Rider, Slow EMA Crossover) — all three turned out to be the same BTC EMA-cross trend edge as DT1, correlating r 0.72–0.82 with it. A library can look deep while holding one real idea repeated. We count edges by their week-by-week correlation, not by their names, which is the only way to avoid stacking four copies of one bet and calling it a diversified book.
  • Asymmetric sizing hides broken legs. Fugu's headline +495% came from risking long trades ~5× harder than shorts. A symmetric 1%-risk test exposed why: the short book actually loses money (−0.165R), so the author's sizing wasn't diversifying — it was muting a dead sleeve. Always test both legs at equal risk before believing a two-sided edge.
  • Short backtest windows manufacture edges. DT24 (Compression-Dwell) posted a plausible PF 2.08 over the author's ~1.5-year window and lost money on all 27 grid cells over our full 5.3 years. A well-coded, no-lookahead strategy with a good short-window number is still the single most common false positive — the whole reason we demand 5.3 years of in-sample before any locked OOS.
  • A statistical pass can be bug-dependent. DT21 cleared every gate and cross-checked exactly — but its edge lives in a short leg carrying two author bugs, and the “fixed” version fails significance. When you fix the code and the edge disappears, the edge was the bug. We keep such a result only as a flagged curiosity, never as a book line.
  • No-stop drawdown flatters even a real edge. DT20's bare crossover carries a genuine, surface-wide significant edge — and still gets disqualified, because with no stop its drawdown is 30.5% and structurally uncapped. A real signal expressed through bad risk management is not tradeable; the stop is not an afterthought, it is what makes the same edge holdable.
