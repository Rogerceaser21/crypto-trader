Monday Breakout Continuation at Weekend SD Levels

The mirror image of the failed SD fade: instead of fading a Monday stretch back to base, trade with it — enter when a 1-minute bar closes beyond the standard-deviation band, ride the extension. 24 pre-declared variants, honest costs, causal levels. Does continuation pay?

Verdict · Not validated

🟡 Marginal, not proven. The best pre-registered variant makes +0.31R per trade (75 trades, 60% win) but at t = 2.33 — under the strict t ≥ 3 bar this idea was set up to clear. It is not a validated edge.

Sixteen of the 24 variants are positive and four reach |t| ≥ 2 — but zero reach t ≥ 3, and this idea was born from a post-hoc "mirror" of a losing strategy, where roughly one variant in 24 is expected to fluke |t| ≥ 2 by chance alone. The best cell does sit inside a coherently-positive neighbourhood (all five one-knob neighbours positive), which is mildly encouraging, but the profit is heavily concentrated in 2020–2021. Treat it as a lead to confirm on the locked 2025–26 out-of-sample data, not as a live edge.

The trade

How the breakout continuation works

Every weekend has a base (the Sunday-night reference close) and, from the history of past weekends, a typical Monday stretch — a mean move and a standard deviation, measured separately for the up side and the down side. We draw a band at base ± (mean + k·SD). During the Monday window (Mon 02:00 → Tue 00:00 Dubai) we wait:

  • When a 1-minute bar closes above the up band → go long on the next bar's open (continue the up-move).
  • When a 1-minute bar closes below the down band → go short (continue the down-move).
  • First close-beyond of the day wins; one trade per weekend. Target sits a further 1 or 2 SD out; the stop sits back toward base (at the mean line, or halfway back). Nothing left open by Tuesday 00:00 is closed at the market.

Every rule tilted against us

Entry uses stop-order fills (long fills at the worse of the band or the next open) plus 0.02% slippage and 0.05% fee each side. If one minute tags both stop and target, it is booked a loss. A stop that gaps through fills at the worse of the stop or that bar's open; a target never gets gap improvement. Risk is the declared band-to-stop distance, so a slipped entry loses slightly more than 1R. Levels are strictly causal — each weekend uses only earlier weekends.

Interaction with the validated fade

Overlap with the Monday fade (descriptive only)

The validated Monday fade (+0.382R over 91 trades, t 2.98) trades the opposite way — it fades the stretch back to base. How often does the same weekend fire both this breakout and the fade? Using the best variant's trigger band (mean+1SD, 3-Month), 29 of the 91 fade weekends also produce a breakout trade.

Sequencing: on 22 weekends the fade tags its (closer) level first and the breakout only fires later when price closes through the further band; on 7 weekends the breakout fires first and the fade then enters against it — literally fading the move this trade is riding. In 25 of 29 overlaps the two are opposite-direction, so they are structurally antagonistic: a weekend that pays the breakout tends to hurt the fade, and vice-versa. Honesty

Why the bar was set so high

Post-hoc origin & the multiple-comparisons trap

This strategy was not an a-priori hypothesis. It came from a post-hoc mirror of the failed SD fade: after the fade lost, a quick "what if we did the opposite" check showed the naive mirror's best cells near +0.25R (t ≈ 2.6) at the mean+1SD band on 3-month stats. Discovering a strategy by inverting a loser, inside the same knob space, badly inflates the odds of fooling yourself.

With 24 variants, pure chance is expected to throw up roughly one cell at |t| ≥ 2 even if there is no real edge. We found four — better than one, and all clustered on the same "target 2×SD" knob, which is mild structure — but none reaches t ≥ 3. So the pre-registered winner bar was deliberately strict: t ≥ 3 AND a coherent same-sign neighbourhood, or the verdict is "not validated." The best cell clears the neighbourhood half (all five neighbours positive) but misses the significance half (t = 2.33). Result: not validated.

Other honest caveats: the profit concentrates in 2020–2021; most exits are time-outs at the Tuesday close, not clean target hits; and the trend overlay that looks best (gated t > 3 on a 4-week cell) is itself a second post-hoc pick and does not help the primary cell. Nothing here has touched the locked 2025–26 out-of-sample data.

Plain English

What this means for you

  • Don't trade this yet. The best version earns a real-looking +0.31R per trade, but at a confidence level (t = 2.33) below the line this test set for itself. Given where the idea came from, that is not enough to believe.
  • The shape is plausible, though. Continuation pays more when you let the target run to 2×SD, it is positive on both long and short breakouts, and every neighbour of the best cell is green. That is a genuine (if faint) ridge, not a lone spike — worth one clean test.
  • The one honest next step is to run this exact best variant — mean+1SD break, 3-Month SD, 2×SD target, structure stop — once on the locked 2025–26 data. If it holds near +0.3R there, it graduates from "lead" to "edge." If it fades, it was the expected fluke.
  • It fights your fade, not complements it. On the 29 weekends both fire, they are almost always opposite-direction. The breakout and the fade are two bets on the same Monday stretch — one that it continues, one that it snaps back — so they can't both be sized up on the same weekend.
