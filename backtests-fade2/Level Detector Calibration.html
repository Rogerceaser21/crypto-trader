Level Detector — Calibration

The causal L1 / L2 / L3 level detector for Annie’s Monday Fade 2, built to Igor’s rulings of 2026‑07‑14 and scored against his 36 hand‑labelled weeks. A level is a volume‑spike cluster of PVSRA vector candles (“a bunch on the volume histogram”) followed by a board‑meeting consolidation. Everything is causal — a level is emitted only once its consolidation is confirmed. This page is the honest scorecard: a pre‑declared 18‑cell knob grid, the best cell, and the per‑week detail — no tuning beyond the declared grid.

Verdict — the level count is still the fuzzy core, and this detector does not crack it.

Best pre‑declared cell (cluster 2 · quiet‑gap 2 · board‑band 2.0%) reaches 64.1% balanced accuracy (61.9% raw) across the pooled 15m‑label + 1H‑label observations — only marginally above the ~60% semantic‑core ceiling flagged in CALIBRATION_V2, and well short of anything you could trade blind. The signal that exists lives almost entirely on the 1H (balanced 66.0%, raw agreement 67.7% vs 45% for the old counter). On the 15m the detector is essentially non‑discriminative (balanced 52.4% ≈ coin‑flip): it prints “3 levels” in ~28 of 32 weeks, so it merely reproduces the 56% positive base‑rate rather than reading the chart. This matches Annie’s own warning that “counting levels is probably the most difficult part of the method.”

1 · What a “level” is (the ruled definition)

  • Level = volume‑spike cluster + consolidation. A cluster is a run of PVSRA vector candles (≥150% class) not separated by a quiet gap of below‑average‑volume bars. Igor’s ruling: the realistic bunch is ~3 vector candles on the 15m (the 1H often shows just 1 for the same event); “6 is impossible — tune down, not up.”
  • Board meeting (the pause that confirms a level): starting values ruled a “good start” — ≥6 bars whose high‑low band stays small while the 50‑EMA slope flattens. In this detector “flattens” is self‑normalising (board‑window slope ≤ 50% of the impulse slope), so there is no magic absolute‑slope constant to over‑fit.
  • L1 → L2 → L3 sequencing. L1 is the cluster that breaks the 50 EMA with a vector, then consolidates; L2 is the next break with PVSRA candles + consolidation; L3 is the strongest final leg. Capped at 3.
  • Timeframe. Count on the 1H by default; switch to the 15m after a vertical 1H leg (single‑bar range ≥ 3× the trailing‑20 1H candle range) that is followed by a ≥8‑bar board meeting — the “damage is done” shortcut. Post‑switch 15m legs continue the trend from the last confirmed 1H level, so the combined L1/L2/L3 sequence stays monotonic.
  • Causal. A level is emitted only when its consolidation is confirmed — a board meeting forms after the impulse, or (in a very strong trend where the pause is skipped) the visibly‑starting next leg is the proof the prior one ended. No look‑ahead.
  • BBWP is dropped by ruling and appears nowhere. PVSRA classes are reused unchanged from the detectors/pvsra.py module (200% = vol ≥ 2× prior‑10 avg; 150% class ≥ 1.5×).

2 · How it was scored

Ground truth is Igor’s workbook tab data/tab_FMWB.csv, columns “3 Levels completed on 15M TF?” and “3 levels on 1H TF?” — the same 36 hand‑labelled weeks CALIBRATION_V2 uses (4 rows excluded for broken/ambiguous dates). Candles are backtests/data/btcusdt_1m.parquet resampled to 15m and 1H exactly as bt_data/bt_weekly do. Per week, the fade direction and entry point (the M/W second peak) come causally from patterns.detect_weekly_pattern on the 1H — the same machinery weekly.extract_week uses — then feed the level detector.

  • 15m label is scored against the pure‑15m count ≥ 3.
  • 1H label is scored against the 1H‑default‑with‑15m‑switch count ≥ 3.
  • The two label columns are pooled (26 positives / 37 negatives across the weeks) into one confusion matrix per cell. Hit‑rate = TP/(TP+FN) on the “3‑levels = yes” weeks; false‑positive rate = FP/(FP+TN); balanced accuracy = ½(hit‑rate + specificity), which strips out the base‑rate inflation the raw agreement suffers on the mostly‑negative 1H column.
Base‑rate honesty. A degenerate detector that always says “3 levels” scores 56% on the 15m column and 26% on the 1H column; one that always says “no” scores 44% / 74%. Both sit at exactly 50% balanced accuracy. That 50% — not 0% — is the number to beat.

On the 15m the detector fires “3 levels” on 11 of 14 no‑weeks (specificity 21%) — it is saying yes almost always. On the 1H it correctly holds back on 16 of 23 no‑weeks (specificity 70%), which is the real source of the pooled edge.

  • The 15m detector adds no discrimination. Balanced 52.4% is a hair above coin‑flip. On the 15m almost every fade week eventually prints three vector clusters, so “3 levels” fires nearly always and the 56% raw agreement is just the positive base‑rate. Igor’s 15m label is not recoverable from mechanical cluster counting.
  • The 1H detector is where the edge is. Balanced 66.0%, raw 67.7% — a genuine lift over the old counter’s 45%. The 1H‑default + vertical‑switch rule earns most of that: it stays conservative in choppy weeks (matching Igor’s many 1H = 0 labels) and escalates to the 15m only when a vertical leg says the damage is done.
  • Ship it as a confidence input, not a gate. A pooled 64% balanced / 49% FP‑rate is a ranked‑ candidate signal, exactly the “scaffold + calibrated‑proxy” posture the Fade 2 spec already prescribes — never a stand‑alone yes/no.

7 · Limitations & honesty

  • There is no per‑level ground truth in the workbook — only the aggregate “did the week show 3 levels”. The individual L1/L2/L3 boundaries, board‑meeting locations and the 15m switch point are unvalidated by column; they can only be eyeballed against Igor’s TradingView snapshots.
  • The direction + entry are inherited from the M/W detector, which is itself near a coin‑flip on Igor’s labels. A wrong direction feeds the level detector a wrong leg, so some misses are upstream, not the counter’s fault.
  • The 15m column is a dead end for mechanical counting (52% balanced). Do not read the 56% raw figure as progress — it is the base‑rate.
  • No cell in the declared grid meaningfully clears ~60% balanced. Per the task’s honesty rule, that is stated plainly: this calibrates the level count as a weak confidence signal, not a reliable gate.
