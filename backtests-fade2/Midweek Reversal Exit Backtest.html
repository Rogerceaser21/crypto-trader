The Midweek Reversal Exit — does the course-faithful exit beat a fixed target?

Annie's rule for closing the Monday fade is not a fixed R:R — it is discretionary: “there is a midweek reversal, usually Wednesday, Thursday, sometimes Friday, where we basically have another M or W… or Final Damage… that's when you're supposed to exit.” That exit was never mechanically tested. Here we hold the frozen validated 91 Monday-spike fades (same entries every time, baseline +0.382R) and replace the 1.9R target with exactly that: exit when an opposite-direction M/W or Final Damage completes midweek on the 1H. Six pre-declared cells. Causal detection (reusing the calibrated patterns.py M/W + Final-Damage logic), realistic fees & slippage, in-sample 2019–2024 only; the locked 2025–26 block is untouched.

✘ NO MECHANICAL EDGE OVER A FIXED TARGET The course-faithful midweek-reversal exit is a real, detectable event — 50–70 of the 91 fades do exit on a genuine opposite M/W/FD, firing Wed/Thu with a few on Friday, exactly as Annie describes — but as a mechanical rule it does not beat simply holding a fixed target. Every one of the six cells lands below the +0.382R baseline (expectancy +0.292R to +0.372R), and every paired-t vs the baseline is negative (best = -0.09, a dead heat, on the V3 no-consolidation runner). Against the F3 winner (fixed 2.5R, +0.506R) it is clearly worse (paired t -2.08 to -1.21). Pre-registered bar was paired t ≥ 2.5 on 6 cells — nothing clears it; nothing even turns positive. Verdict: keep the fixed target. The midweek reversal is meaningful as a discretionary read, not as a mechanical exit — it takes profit too early and gives back the reversion edge the fixed target keeps.

1 · What the midweek reversal exit is (plain English)

The Monday fade enters against a Sun/Mon spike out of the weekend box: a spike up is faded short (an M / top formed), a spike down is faded long (a W / bottom formed). Annie then holds the trade until the market prints the opposite reversal in the middle of the week — another M/W (or a Final Damage fake-out) — and exits there, rather than at a mechanical R multiple.

  • Long trade → exit on a TOP (M / FDM). A short trade → exit on a BOTTOM (W / FDW). The exit pattern is the mirror of the entry, so it marks the point the favourable move is done.
  • Detected causally on the 1H with the same building blocks the entry detector uses: a k=3 fractal peak-2 “spike-out”, a prominent same-side peak-1 in a ~2-day lookback, and the exact patterns.py classifier (peak-2 vicinity band MAX_SHORTFALL = 0.35; a lower-high inside the band = M/W, an overshoot inside the band that closes back in = Final Damage, anything wider = a breakout and ignored).
  • No look-ahead. A fractal peak is only knowable 3 bars later, so the “completion bar” is peak + 3; the completion bar must be timestamped Wed 00:00 New York or later and after entry; the exit fills at the open of the next 15m bar after the 1H completion bar closes, with adverse slippage.
  • Everything else stays the flagship: the baseline hard stop is kept, the 1.9R target is dropped (the level exit replaces it), the refund rule is OFF, and the Friday UK-session cap is the fallback if no midweek reversal ever forms.

5 · When the midweek exit actually fires (by day)

This is the part that validates Annie's description: of the fades that close on a midweek reversal, the completion bar lands overwhelmingly on Wednesday and Thursday, with a handful on Friday — her exact words. The Wed-00:00-NY gate guarantees nothing fires earlier. So the mechanism is faithful and the timing is real; it is only the P&L that fails to improve on a fixed target.

7 · Honest verdict & caveats

What this does and does not say

  • The midweek reversal is faithfully captured and behaves exactly as described (Wed/Thu-heavy, occasional Friday). This is a genuine, causal, mechanically-detectable event — not a fudge.
  • It does not beat a fixed target. All six cells sit below +0.382R with negative paired-t; the pre-registered bar (paired t ≥ 2.5) is not cleared by any, and none even turns positive — so the multiple-comparisons correction across the 6 cells is moot: there is no candidate win to discount.
  • Why it underperforms: the opposite M/W/FD typically completes after the reversion has already run past 1.9R but before it reaches the 2.5–3R zone the F3 sweep found optimal — so it systematically books winners a bit early while doing nothing to cut losers (the hard stop still governs those). Adding Final Damage (V2) worsens this; only the no-consolidation filter (V3) recovers the difference back to a tie.
  • Drawdown is higher than the 1.9R baseline (up to 6.9R vs 4.6R) for the runner cells — you pay more variance for the same money. The 50%-partial overlay trims that (down to ~4.1–4.7R) and lifts win-rate, but at slightly lower expectancy: a variance play, not an edge gain.
  • Scope: in-sample 2019–2024 on the frozen entries; BTC-USDT perp; the locked 2025–26 out-of-sample block was not touched. No OOS run is warranted — there is no promotable challenger here.

The take

Trade the fixed target, keep the midweek reversal as a discretionary overlay. If you are watching the chart and a clean opposite M/W/FD prints Wed/Thu, taking profit there is reasonable — but the numbers say a mechanical 1.9R (or the F3-preferred 2.5R) target does at least as well with less work and less drawdown. The midweek reversal earns its place in the playbook narrative, not in the exit rule.
