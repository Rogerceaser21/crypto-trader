Section 2 · ✅ Validated

The winner: the Monday FMWB fade

What it is: the Monday FMWB fade is Annie's flagship weekly setup, mechanised. Each week the market makers spike price out of the weekend range early — a Sunday/Monday stop-hunt — and then reverse; the trade fades that Monday spike back toward where the real move began. It is a once-a-week, structural stop-hunt fade with a wide stop at the week's extreme and a fixed 1.9R target.

The rules, as a checklist you could follow

  1. Draw the weekend box from the Friday 5pm-NY close-clusters (bodies, not wicks) through to Sunday 5pm NY.
  2. Wait for a Sun/Mon spike-out of that box, with the week's extreme wick printed on Monday. No Monday spike → no trade that week.
  3. Confirm an M / W / Final-Damage reversal on the 1H, with peak-2 in the vicinity of peak-1.
  4. On the 15m, require a 200%-class PVSRA vector whose body closes across the 50 EMA — that is your Level 1.
  5. Take the retest entry when 2 of {50-EMA wick-touch & reject · price back into the L1 vector range · higher-low / lower-high structure} line up (heatmap/OI is the optional 4th, once you have Coinalyze).
  6. Enter at the next 15m open, fading the spike: spike up → short, spike down → long.
  7. Stop beyond the week's spike extreme (the Monday HOD/LOD wick) plus a small buffer.
  8. Target = 1.9R fixed. The literal “TP at the L1-vector origin” sits too close to that stop to be tradeable (median 0.58 R:R) — use the 1.9R minimum-as-target instead.
  9. Hard-close by Friday UK-session end. Never enter after Friday 5pm NY, and never on weekends.
  10. Trade one unit, held to TP / stop / Friday close. Optional risk overlays (partials at channels, breakeven after L2) only reduce variance.

Cross-engine confirmation — the strongest evidence hereAll 119 Monday trades (91 in-sample + 28 out-of-sample) were re-run through a completely separate backtesting engine (Jesse). It took every one of the same trades, closed each for the same reason, and won or lost on exactly the same weeks: 119 / 119 matched, PASS. The tiny R difference (~0.018R) was 100% the reference engine's slippage charge, which Jesse doesn't model. The edge is a property of the price action and the rules — not a bug in the custom fill code.
Monte-Carlo drawdownShuffling the Monday-fade trade order 5,000 times, the median worst drawdown was ~5R (about 5% of the account) and the 95th-percentile worst was ~8R (~8%). The chance of a drawdown of 20% or more was 0.0%. Because shuffling only reorders trades, the total profit is identical in every shuffle — the shape of this edge is robust to bad luck in sequencing.
Honest caveats — read these before you size up
  • The full-sample edge is thin — Monday-only is the trade. Across all 223 weekly fades the expectancy is only +0.099R (t = 1.23, not statistically clean). Almost all of the money lives in the 91 Monday-spike weeks. Without the Monday filter, there is no bankable edge.
  • Year concentration. In-sample, 2022 and 2024 carried the profit (those two years alone were +27R); 2021 and 2023 were losing years. The edge is real but uneven across regimes.
  • Low frequency. Roughly 1.5 signals a month. This is a patient, once-a-week trade, not a turnover machine.
  • Direction is still improvable. The mechanical fade matches your discretionary M/W direction call only ~50% of the time. Mechanically forcing a regime flip made it worse. The missing half of the edge is the direction read — the judgement the geometry can't recover — which is exactly what the gallery + OI work is for.

Section 3 · 🟡 Marginal

The one maybe: 3-Hits to a level

3-Hits is the only other setup that finished in the black — and only just. When the weekly high or low is tested three times cleanly and then breaks its rejection trendline, fading it earned +0.108R over 37 in-sample trades (WR 45.9%, PF 1.18) and held its sign out-of-sample (+0.05R). But the t-statistic is 0.48 — statistically indistinguishable from zero — and the edge only survives on the tightest exact-touch definition of a “hit”; loosening the tolerance turns it negative. It behaves like a real, small, structural level-reversal, distinct from the Monday fade.

Verdict: a candidate to size very small or use as confluence alongside the Monday fade — not a validated standalone edge. The natural next test is its second named trigger, the 200-EMA rejection variant, which wasn't run.

Section 4 · ❌ Dead

The graveyard

The meta-lessonEvery naked counter-trend trigger lost. Brinks, Final Damage, Mid-Week Reversal and the Backflip flip are all bets that price reverses on a candle shape or a level test — and mechanically, the completed trend continued more often than it cleanly reversed. The only place the money was is the weekly Monday structure: a once-a-week structural stop-hunt, faded in the direction you already teach. The crisp candle needs the context wrapped around it; the context is the edge, not the candle.
