Why the Monday Fade Works

The mechanism behind the edge — an evidence-ranked explanation, not a backtest · BTC-perp, in-sample 2019-09-09 → 2024-12-31 UTC (277 weekends) · own-data checks + cited literature · weekend = Friday 17:00 New York → Monday 02:00 Dubai.

The short answer. The Monday fade works because the weekend is a liquidity vacuum: with the CME and the US index-futures shut from Friday 17:00 NY, BTC drifts and overshoots its weekend range on thin order books — but the Friday-close price stays a magnet. When CME and US index futures reopen Sunday 18:00 NY, real two-way flow floods back in a sharp 3-hour volatility burst (in our data the hottest hour of the whole weekend, t = +9) and drags price back toward the Friday close / weekend base. Our own candles reproduce the documented “CME gap” fill curve almost exactly — 67.9% of weekend gaps fill within 48h (published figure ≈67%), 79.4% by the following Friday (≈77%), 95.7% eventually. The trade does not fade the stretch itself (fading naked stretch levels loses every variant). It fades the failed breakout — the instant a thin-book push above the weekend range gets rejected because returning liquidity refuses to follow. That rejection is the tell that the overshoot was a liquidity grab, not real demand, and it makes the base a target. So the edge = weekend vacuum (why it overshoots) + CME/TradFi return (why and when it snaps back) + gap magnetism (where it snaps back to), with the failed-breakout filter separating the grabs from the genuine trends. The classic “Mondays are green” seasonality is not the driver — on our sample it is statistically indistinguishable from noise.

How to read this

Evidence grades. Every mechanism carries a grade for how well the evidence supports it — not how good the story sounds.

GradeMeaning
A · confirmedConfirmed in our own IS data with an executable, deterministic check.
B · citedSupported by a credible external study (peer-reviewed paper, exchange research desk, or a quant write-up with stated methodology).
C · theoryTheory or trader folklore — plausible, widely repeated, but without hard quantitative proof (or not checkable on our data).

Plain-language notes. “CME gap” = the price jump between where CME Bitcoin futures closed Friday and where they reopen Sunday evening, because spot/perp keeps trading 24/7 while CME is shut. “Gap fill” = price later trading back to the Friday-close level. “Failed breakout” = price pokes above (or below) the weekend range then gets rejected back inside. “t” = t-statistic; with ~277 weekends, |t| < 2 is noise, |t| ≥ 2 clears the usual bar for a single test.

The one picture: our weekend gaps fill on the documented schedule

For all 277 in-sample weekends we measured the CME weekend gap (Friday 17:00 NY close price vs the Sunday 18:00 NY reopen price) and asked by when does price trade back through the Friday-close level. The bars are our data; the orange markers are the independent published fill rates. They land on top of each other.

What this means. The Friday-close price is not an arbitrary level — it behaves as a genuine attractor, on the same timetable third parties document for the CME gap. That is the quantitative backbone of “the base is a target.” Median absolute gap size on our sample was 1.19% (90th percentile 4.55%); the median time from the Sunday reopen to the fill was 14 hours — i.e. price typically returns to the Friday level during Monday, which is exactly the fade’s playing field.

Mechanism cards, ranked by evidence

1CME weekend-gap magnetism — the Friday-close price is a targetGRADE A

CME Bitcoin futures traded on a Sunday-evening–to–Friday-afternoon schedule for the whole of our in-sample window (they only went 24/7 on 2026-05-29, well after our data ends). They closed Friday 16:00 Chicago (=17:00 New York) and reopened Sunday 17:00 Chicago (=18:00 New York). Because spot and perps keep trading through that ~25-hour blackout, the reopen almost always prints away from the Friday close — the “gap.” The well-worn observation is that these gaps tend to fill (price comes back to the Friday level). We tested it on our own candles and it holds, matching the published numbers.

Our data (A)
277/277 weekends had a gap. Filled: 29.6% by end of the Sunday burst, 58.1% by end of Monday, 67.9% within 48h, 79.4% by the next Friday, 95.7% ever. Down-gaps filled 100% of the time eventually (up-gaps 92.9%). Small gaps (<0.5%) filled 97% by next Friday; big gaps (≥2%) only 58.9%.
Literature (B)
~77% of CME BTC gaps (2018–2026) eventually fill; ~67% within 48h (2022–2025); gaps <$500 fill ~85% in 1–2 weeks and <$700 ~92% within 30 trading days; CoinDesk counted 79 of 80 recent gaps filled before the 24/7 switch.
What would kill this: if a placebo level the same distance away got touched just as often (i.e. fills are ordinary volatility/mean-reversion, not CME-specific) — and if fading-to-the-base doesn’t actually improve realised R over a fixed target. Also watch the post-2026 24/7 regime: if snap-backs persist with no gap, the “gap” framing was cosmetic and the real cause is the liquidity/flow story in cards 2–3.
Implication for the trade: the weekend base / Friday-close level is a real magnet → use levels-as-targets exits, bank profit at the base rather than trailing forever. But scale ambition with stretch size: on ≥2% overshoots only ~59% fill by Friday, so don’t hold the whole position for a full fill on the biggest stretches.
2Weekend liquidity vacuum → TradFi-return volatility burstGRADE A / B

Weekends are thin. Institutional desks and the ETF/CME complex step back, so order books hollow out and the same order size moves price further. Price wanders. Then the US session returns — and the single sharpest, most reliable event in the whole weekend is the burst right at the Sunday 18:00–21:00 NY window, exactly when CME crypto and the S&P/Nasdaq index futures reopen. That is when the fuel to snap the overshoot back arrives.

Our data (A)
The Sunday 18:00–21:00 NY hour averages 0.528% absolute move vs 0.320% for a typical weekend hour — Welch t=+9.0, the hottest window of the weekend (the run-in, Sun 16:00–18:00 NY, is also elevated, t=+3.8). Direction is a coin-flip; only the energy spikes. The window is locked to New-York wall-clock, i.e. to the US session, not to any Dubai/UTC hour.
Literature (B)
Kaiko: weekend share of BTC volume fell from ~24–25% (2018) to ~13–16% (2024); weekday volume now runs ~2× weekend. BridgePort: on weekends bid-ask spreads widen ~11%, effective depth for a $100k trade drops ~9%, displayed liquidity ~5%. Thinner books → larger % moves from smaller orders.
What would kill this: if an identical volatility burst showed up at 18:00 NY on random weekdays (then it isn’t the weekend-vacuum-plus-TradFi-return that matters, just a daily US-open effect) — or if the burst tracked a Dubai/UTC clock rather than the New-York session. Neither is what we see.
Implication for the trade: don’t fade into the vacuum before TradFi returns — the snap-back fuel arrives with the Sunday 18:00–21:00 NY burst. Time entries around/after that window; the burst is the catalyst that turns a stretched overshoot into a reversion.
3The failed breakout is a liquidity grab / stop-hunt — not real demandGRADE A obs. / B–C mech.

This is the heart of the edge and the subtlest part. Fading the stretch as such loses. What pays is fading the failure: the weekend push above the range gets rejected, on a big-volume candle, back through the moving average. The interpretation — Annie’s thesis, and the “Sunday scam-wick” folklore — is that thin books let price be run into the resting stop/liquidation orders above the range (a liquidity grab), and once that fuel is spent with no genuine buyers behind it, price collapses back. The rejection is the signature of a grab; a breakout that holds had real demand and should not be faded.

Our data (A, for the observation)
Fading naked statistical-stretch levels lost in all 48 variants tested. Joining a held breakout was borderline-positive (+0.31R, t=2.3). Fading the failed breakout returned +0.382R over 91 Monday trades in-sample and +0.54R out-of-sample. So the edge lives specifically in the failure signal, not the stretch — consistent with “the overshoot was a grab.”
Literature (B–C, for the mechanism)
BitMEX’s own research (“Scamwicks and Stop Cascades”) lays out the order-book mechanics: in a thin book one side gets swept, the last price gaps to the next resting orders, tripping stops/liquidations in a cascade that reverses once exhausted. Trader-education and liquidation-heatmap tools (Coinglass) show stop clusters pooling just above range highs. Weekend breakouts are widely cited as failing ~70–80% of the time in thin conditions. The specific “smart-money hunts your stop” framing (ICT liquidity sweep) is folklore (Grade C); the order-book cascade is documented (Grade B).
What would kill this: if fading held breakouts paid as well as fading failed ones — our data already refutes that. The live risk is regime: if weekend liquidity keeps deepening (post-ETF, and after CME’s 2026 move to 24/7), grabs may get rarer and the failure edge could fade. Monitor it out-of-sample.
Implication for the trade: the entry trigger must be a confirmed failure — a rejection candle with a ~200%-of-average volume spike pushing back through the 50-EMA — not merely reaching a stretch level. This filter is the edge; protect it and never loosen it into a naked stretch-fade.
4Classic Monday / weekend seasonality — context, not causeGRADE B lit. / not our driver

There is a real academic literature on day-of-week and weekend effects in crypto. But on our sample the “Mondays are green” daily seasonality is not significant, and the trade’s direction is a coin-flip — so this is background colour, not the engine. Importantly, the most careful intraday work agrees with us: the daily effect largely evaporates once you look hour-by-hour, surviving only as a narrow burst when US participants log back on.

Our data (A)
Monday close-to-close mean is +0.415% (t=+1.68) on UTC days, +0.321% (t=+1.29) on Dubai days — both noise (|t|<2). No weekday clears the multiple-test bar. The Monday edge is therefore not a daily-return anomaly; it is intraday and structural.
Literature (B)
Caporale & Plastun (2019) find day-of-week anomalies in crypto (strongest early-sample); Kaya/Aydemir report Bitcoin Monday returns significantly higher with elevated Mon/Thu volatility; a 2025 study finds negative weekend crypto returns predict Monday equity declines (asymmetric, loss-aversion). A quant re-examination (mlquants) shows the daily effect mostly vanishes intraday except a Sunday 23:00–00:00 UTC burst as US retail returns — the same “price discovery restarts when the crowd comes back” story as our card 2.
What would kill this as a cause: nothing to kill — it is already not the cause on our sample. The value here is negative knowledge: it tells us the edge is not seasonal drift.
Implication for the trade: do not lean on “Mondays go up.” Trade the structure (failed breakout → base) in whichever direction the failure points; keep the play direction-agnostic.
5Funding-rate weekend patterns on perpsGRADE C

Perp funding is a real, periodic force, but a clean weekend-specific funding pattern that would drive the Monday fade is not well documented, and we can’t test it here (our candle file has no funding column). Best treated as a possible minor tailwind, not a pillar.

What is documented (B)
Funding is exchanged every 8 hours at 00:00 / 08:00 / 16:00 UTC. Extreme positive funding (>0.10% per 8h) tends to precede corrections (crowded longs); extreme negative precedes short-squeezes. But the raw funding→next-return link is weak (Fulgur Ventures: R²≈0.003).
What is speculative (C)
The idea that funding predictably flattens over the weekend (leverage demand drops with liquidity) and that imbalances build into Monday is repeated by desks but not cleanly quantified in the sources found; behaviour looks conditional on the broader regime rather than a fixed weekly cycle.
What would kill this: if measured weekend funding is flat/uninformative relative to weekdays, or adds nothing on top of cards 1–3 in a joint test, the mechanism is moot.
Implication for the trade: at most a confluence flag — if a weekend overshoot coincides with extreme funding in the overshoot’s direction, the failed-breakout fade has extra reason to work (crowded, forced to unwind). Do not size on it; treat as unproven until we pull a funding series and check.

Our-data check — the CME weekend-gap fill test

The Grade-A backbone (card 1, and the fill-curve chart) comes from a single deterministic script, weekend_gap_check.py, run over the in-sample window only (2019-09-09 → 2024-12-31). It is a pure function of the candles: no randomness, no wall-clock, no run-order dependence. Two runs produce a byte-identical output CSV (verified by md5, below).

Method (exactly what the script does)

  • Anchors (pinned in New-York local time so US daylight-saving is handled by the tz database): Friday CME close = 17:00 NY; Sunday CME reopen = 18:00 NY. The brief’s phrase “Monday CME-open-time price” resolves to this Sunday-evening reopen — the CME’s first print of the new week, and the print the gap is measured against.
  • Gap = reopen price − Friday-close price (using the 1-minute close as-of each anchor). Up-gap if the weekend rallied while CME was shut, down-gap if it sold off.
  • Fill = the first 1-minute bar after the reopen whose [low, high] range straddles the Friday-close price (price trades back through that level).
  • Fill-by-when horizons, chronological from the reopen: Sun 21:00 NY (+3h) end of Monday (Tue 00:00 Dubai) reopen + 48h next Fri 17:00 NY ever in IS.
  • Note on the weekend window: its close (Monday 02:00 Dubai = Sunday 22:00 UTC) coincides with the CME reopen — so the “weekend window” ends exactly when CME comes back, which is why the first meaningful fill horizon is +3h, not something inside the weekend.

What’s still speculation (be honest)

  • Causation vs correlation on the gap. We confirmed gaps fill on schedule, but not that the CME gap causes the fill rather than being one label for ordinary volatility mean-reversion. A placebo-level test (does a random level the same distance away get touched as often?) is not yet run.
  • The stop-hunt/liquidity-grab story is an interpretation. Our data proves the failed-breakout filter adds edge; it does not prove why (resting-stop cascades). That the mechanism is the failure, not the stretch, is solid; that the failure is literally a stop-run is inference supported by exchange order-book write-ups, not by our candles.
  • Funding (card 5) is essentially untested here — no funding series in the candle file, and the weekend-specific periodicity is weakly documented. Treat as a hypothesis.
  • Regime shift risk. ETF-era deepening and CME’s move to 24/7 (2026-05-29) remove the literal weekend gap and thin the vacuum. The mechanism could weaken going forward; the +0.54R out-of-sample result is encouraging but the causal chain must be re-checked on fresh data.
  • Direction remains a coin-flip. Nothing here gives an edge on which way Monday goes — only on fading confirmed failures back to the base.

Implications — how Igor should trade it

  1. Treat the base as a real target, not hope. The Friday-close / weekend-base level fills on a documented schedule (68% in 48h, 79% by Friday) → exit at the base (levels-as-targets), don’t over-trail.
  2. Scale target ambition to stretch size. Small overshoots almost always fill; on ≥2% stretches only ~59% fill by Friday → take partials earlier / don’t demand a full fill on the biggest moves.
  3. Enter with the Sunday burst, not before it. The snap-back catalyst is the Sunday 18:00–21:00 NY reopen burst. Don’t fade into the dead weekend; wait for TradFi flow to arrive.
  4. Require a confirmed failure, always. Rejection of the range with a ~200%-volume candle back through the 50-EMA is the edge. Never degrade the trigger into a naked stretch-fade — that loses.
  5. Stay direction-agnostic. Ignore “Mondays are green” (noise on our data). Fade the failure whichever way it points.
  6. Use funding only as optional confluence. Extreme weekend funding in the overshoot’s direction is a “nice-to-have” tell of a crowded, unwind-prone book — never a sizing input until tested.
  7. Watch the regime. Re-run the gap check and the failed-breakout edge on 2025–26 data periodically; if deepening liquidity / CME 24/7 kills the fill schedule, the mechanism — and the edge — is eroding.
