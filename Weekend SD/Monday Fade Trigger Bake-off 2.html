Monday Fade — Trigger Bake-off 2

Can other indicators sharpen the validated Monday fade's entry — as extra filters, or as replacement confirmations? Fifteen published exhaustion / mean-reversion tools were declared from source, frozen, then run once against the 91 validated fades.

Verdict

❌ No indicator improves the fade. The PVSRA failed-breakout confirmation stands alone.

Of 18 declared comparisons (15 filters + 3 replacements), not one filter's kept-vs-dropped Welch t reaches ±2 — no tool separates the fade's winners from its losers. The filters that meaningfully thin the sample mostly keep the worse trades (negative Welch t); the two that nudge average-R up (RSI-divergence, PMARP) do so only by shrinking N, are statistically indistinguishable from the trades they drop, and are exactly what you expect from luck. Every replacement (indicator band-cross swapped in for the PVSRA retest) is decisively worse (avg −0.12R to −0.66R, t as low as −5.4).

The signal underneath the null: the edge lives in the retest / pullback mechanics (wait for the failed breakout to confirm and pull back), not in which oscillator flags the overshoot. A weekend spike-out is, by construction, already an extreme on every short-lookback indicator — so they add no selectivity. Keep the fade exactly as validated: +0.382R, 91 trades, t = 2.98.

The question

What this tested

Baseline to beat — the validated Annie Monday fade: weekend-box spike-out → M/W peak-2 → a 200% PVSRA vector body-closes through the 50-EMA → 2-of-3 retest → fade, TP 1.9R, stop beyond the extreme, Friday close. On the Monday subset that is +0.382R over 91 trades, t = 2.98 (in-sample 2019-09 → 2024-12-31). The mechanism established in R1: the edge is the failed-breakout confirmation after a thin-weekend overshoot — the weekend base acts as a magnet.

Two ways an indicator could help, both declared up front:

  • Filter Keep only the fades where indicator X also printed its published extreme, on the fade side, during the Monday overshoot we are fading. Does the sub-sample beat the whole?
  • Replacement For the three band-type tools that define a price trigger, let the indicator's band-cross replace the PVSRA confirmation as the entry — same weekends, same direction, same exits (TP 1.9R, stop beyond the running extreme, Friday close).

One strict causality rule for every candidate

All indicators are computed on the 15-minute series (the fade's native timeframe). For each fade, a filter may only use bars at or before the entry. Because the fade enters on a retest — after the spike has already retraced — momentum has normalised by the entry bar itself. So each exhaustion filter asks the mechanically correct question: "did indicator X reach its published extreme, on the fade side, at some completed 15m bar during the Monday overshoot, up to (not including) the entry bar?" The window is the Monday session, causally truncated at entry. This window is dictated by the fade's retest mechanism, not chosen to favour any candidate; thresholds are unchanged and taken from source. (A first pass that read each indicator only at the single entry-retest bar left almost every cell empty — 0–2 trades — precisely because the extreme is gone by the retest. That near-vacuous result is itself confirmation that the entry sits well past the exhaustion point.)

Entering at the raw overshoot extreme — the moment price pokes the band — with a stop just beyond that extreme is the opposite of what the fade does. Honesty box — comparison count & luck math

Declared comparisons: 15 filters + 3 replacements = 18, all frozen before results, run in a single pass, no post-hoc additions, no threshold tuning.

Expected false positives: at two-sided |t| ≥ 2 (p ≈ 0.046), pure luck yields ≈ 18 × 0.046 ≈ 0.8 comparisons (about one). Several filter kept-t values exceed 2 — but that is not independent evidence: those filters keep ~all 91 trades and therefore inherit the baseline's t ≈ 2.98. The decision statistic is the kept-vs-dropped Welch t (does the filter actually separate winners from losers?), and its maximum across all 15 filters is +1.02 — below the ≈1 false positive luck would hand us, and nowhere near the ±2 significance line.

Winner bar (declared): t ≥ 3 with N ≥ 40 from a genuinely discriminating filter, OR ≥ 2 unrelated indicators agreeing significantly on the same filtered subset. Not met. C10's nominal kept-t of 3.02 is an artefact — it drops only 4 of 91 trades (a −0.04R tail), so it is the baseline in disguise, not a discriminating filter. No two indicators agree with significance.

Thin cells (N < 20): none — after the overshoot-window design the smallest kept sample is N = 39 (C14). No row was headlined on a thin cell.

So what

What this means for the trade

Trade the fade exactly as validated. Add nothing. No published exhaustion or mean-reversion indicator — nor any of our four validated Krown replicas — improves the entry as a filter, and every one tested as a replacement makes it worse.

  • Why no filter helps. The setup is the extreme. A thin-weekend spike-out already registers as overbought/oversold on essentially every short-lookback tool, so requiring a second extreme adds no information — it either keeps everything (RSI2, CCI, Keltner, ATR-stretch) or, when it does thin the sample, thins it at random (Welch t inside ±1.6, mostly negative).
  • Where the edge really lives. The replacement test is decisive: swapping the PVSRA retest for an indicator band-touch collapses the edge. The value is in the failed-breakout-then-pullback mechanics — waiting for the overshoot to fail and price to return to the level — not in which oscillator lit up. That matches the R1 mechanism (the weekend base is a magnet; the fade profits from the return, not from calling the top).
  • The two "nice-looking" filters are traps. CT RSI-divergence (60% win, 0.43R) and PMARP look better only because they shrink N; their kept-vs-dropped separation is insignificant (+0.43, +0.42) and their kept-t falls below the baseline. Adopting either would be curve-fitting to noise and would cost roughly half the trades.
  • Housekeeping. The Krown "Advanced Stochastic" (C16) could not be tested honestly — its oscillator construction is a proprietary MISMATCH, not recoverable from OHLC — so the standard Lane stochastic (C6) stood in; it, too, added nothing. True order-flow / volume-delta divergence is not proxyable from OHLCV (no aggressor side), so C11 used an OBV-slope proxy, which was the worst-separating filter of all (−1.60).

Scope: single in-sample pass (2019-09 → 2024-12-31), BTC-USDT perp, 15m native, causal-only, fees 5 bps/side + 2 bps slip/fill. Deterministic: both output CSVs are md5-identical across two runs. This is an expected and clean null — the validated fade needs no confirmation crutch.
